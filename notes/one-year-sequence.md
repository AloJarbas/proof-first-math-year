# One-year sequence for durable mathematical maturity

The strongest one-year sequence is:

1. proof foundations first,
2. linear algebra in two passes,
3. real analysis as the central maturity-builder,
4. probability after proof and analysis habits are in place,
5. a short consolidation block for review, rewrite, and integration with scientific interests.

The key design choice is not to start with probability. Probability becomes much more durable after comfort with proofs, vector-space structure, convergence, and careful notation.

## Why this order

- Proof first reduces friction everywhere else.
- Linear algebra gives structure early and connects directly to coding, optics, signal processing, and modeling.
- Analysis is the main engine of mathematical maturity because it forces precise control of limits, continuity, approximation, and hypotheses.
- Probability lands better on a stronger base.

## Year plan

### Phase 1: proof bootstrapping, weeks 1-8

Primary source: Hammack, *Book of Proof*.

Focus:
- logic, sets, functions,
- direct proof, contradiction, contrapositive,
- induction,
- writing short clean proofs.

Output:
- a proof journal with 4-6 finished proofs per week,
- a personal error list covering quantifier slips, hidden assumptions, algebra gaps, and notation mistakes.

### Phase 2: linear algebra, intuition then structure, weeks 9-24

Primary sources: MIT 18.06 plus Axler, *Linear Algebra Done Right*.

Order:
- weeks 9-14 for systems, matrices, eigenideas, geometry, and computational fluency,
- weeks 15-24 for vector spaces, linear maps, eigenvalues, inner products, and operators.

### Phase 3: real analysis as maturity core, weeks 25-40

Primary source: MIT 18.100A Real Analysis.

Focus:
- sequences and series,
- continuity and differentiability,
- Riemann integration,
- sequences and series of functions,
- uniform convergence and interchange of limits.

### Phase 4: probability on a mature base, weeks 41-48

Primary source: Blitzstein and Hwang through Stat 110.

Focus:
- conditioning,
- independence,
- random variables and distributions,
- expectation and variance,
- standard families and approximations,
- model-building before calculation.

### Phase 5: consolidation and integration, weeks 49-52

Use this block to revisit weak proof topics, redo selected linear algebra and analysis problems without notes, and write one integrative essay or notebook entry linking the year back to scientific work.

## Weekly rhythm

A good default is 6 study days per week, 90-120 minutes per day.

- 3 days for main new material,
- 2 days for problem sets or proof writing,
- 1 day for review and rewrite from memory.

Rule: for every hour of reading, spend at least one hour doing problems or rewriting proofs.
