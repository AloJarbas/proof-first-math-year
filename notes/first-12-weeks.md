# First 12 weeks: proof first, then controlled expansion

Use a proof-dominant ramp rather than four parallel full courses.

- Weeks 1-4: proof is the spine.
- Weeks 5-8: proof remains primary and calculus begins as a controlled secondary stream.
- Weeks 9-12: add discrete mathematics and a light linear-algebra preview without letting them crowd out proof practice.

## Weeks 1-4

Main work:
- proof methods,
- definitions and examples rewritten by hand,
- 2-4 finished proofs per week,
- one weekly review of mistake patterns.

Why: the proof habit is the hardest bottleneck and should not be diluted immediately.

## Weeks 5-8

Main work:
- keep the proof journal active,
- begin single-variable calculus using MIT OCW independent-study materials,
- use calculus for disciplined problem volume, not for abandoning proof writing.

Why: calculus adds momentum and scientific relevance, but should not displace the proof habit too early.

## Weeks 9-12

Main work:
- continue the proof journal,
- continue calculus,
- begin discrete mathematics for formal definitions, proof style, and combinatorial structure,
- add only a light linear-algebra preview, not a full heavy block.

Why: discrete math reinforces proof language. Linear algebra matters, but starting it too heavily before the proof routine stabilizes risks fragmentation.

## Weekly shape from week 9 onward

- proof or journal work: 3 sessions,
- calculus: 2 sessions,
- discrete math: 1-2 sessions,
- linear algebra preview: 1 light session,
- 1 weekly review block.

## What this avoids

- pretending to run four equal-intensity math courses at once,
- turning proof practice into vague reading,
- delaying calculus so long that scientific momentum disappears,
- starting linear algebra so hard that everything becomes fragmented.
