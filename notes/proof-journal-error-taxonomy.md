# Proof journal error taxonomy and entry gates

Track error types, not just finished pages. Then use simple entry gates before calculus, discrete mathematics, or linear algebra are allowed to grow.

## Error taxonomy

### E1: statement parsing
You misread what must be shown, what is assumed, or which direction the implication runs.

### E2: quantifier drift
You lost track of “for all,” “there exists,” or the order in which choices are allowed.

### E3: definition weakness
You could not unpack a definition cleanly enough to start the proof.

### E4: proof-form mismatch
You chose the wrong proof method.

### E5: algebra or symbolic sloppiness
The idea was fine, but manipulation, notation, or bookkeeping broke the proof.

### E6: set or function language weakness
Images, preimages, subsets, equality of sets, or function properties became fuzzy.

### E7: induction control failure
Base case, induction hypothesis use, or step structure was incomplete or poorly targeted.

### E8: counterexample blindness
A false claim survived too long because no concrete test case was tried early.

## Weekly review questions

At the end of each week, ask:
- Which error tag appeared most?
- Which tag cost the most time?
- Which tag is merely sloppy, and which signals conceptual weakness?
- Which one repair action would most reduce next week’s friction?

The journal should not only say “I solved three proofs.” It should say what kind of thinker I failed to be this week.

## Entry gates for widening the load

### Gate A: calculus may stay light but active when
- at least 6 finished proofs exist across two weeks,
- no single week is dominated by E1 or E3,
- the proof journal is actually being kept.

### Gate B: discrete mathematics may expand when
- proof writing no longer collapses on definitions every session,
- E2 and E4 are no longer constant red flags,
- one timed problem block per week is sustainable.

### Gate C: linear algebra should remain preview-only until
- two consecutive weeks are at least yellow-to-green under the existing review protocol,
- proof throughput is steady,
- algebraic sloppiness is not drowning the core work.

If the gates are not met, narrowing is progress. The point is durable proof fluency, not curricular vanity.
