# Proof-study loop: retrieval, worked examples, and interleaving

If proof study is going badly, the problem is often not ambition alone.
The loop is wrong.

A better default loop is:

1. retrieve the definition or theorem from memory,
2. study one worked proof and explain each move in your own words,
3. solve one near-transfer problem without looking,
4. mix that problem with older proof types,
5. log the miss if you stalled, guessed, or used the wrong move.

## Why this loop is better than repeated cold attempts

Cold proof attempts matter.
They are just expensive when the proof form is still new.

A novice who has not yet internalized the moves in a contradiction proof, induction proof, or epsilon-style argument can burn a lot of effort without learning the structure cleanly.
A worked proof gives the shape.
Self-explanation keeps it from turning into passive copying.
A near-transfer attempt checks whether the structure actually stuck.
Interleaving with older proof types forces the real skill: choosing the right move instead of repeating the last one seen.

## Where to use it

Use this loop when you are learning a new proof method, a new style of argument, or a theorem family with unfamiliar setup.

Good fits:

- contradiction,
- contrapositive,
- induction,
- divisibility arguments,
- set identities,
- quantifier-heavy statements.

## Where not to overuse it

Do not live inside worked examples forever.
If every session is just reading polished proofs, you will mistake recognition for control.

The examples should be front-loaded and then faded.
Once a proof type is somewhat familiar, shift the balance toward independent attempts, mixed review, and error correction.

## A compact weekly shape

For one proof topic:

- 1 session: retrieve definitions and theorem statements from memory
- 1 session: study 1-2 worked proofs with self-explanations
- 2 sessions: solve near-transfer and mixed proof problems
- 1 review block: classify errors in the proof journal

## Failure signals

The loop needs fixing if:

- you can follow a proof but cannot start a similar one,
- you keep choosing the wrong proof method,
- every session feels new again after a few days,
- the proof journal fills with the same mistake pattern.

## Source basis

Accepted:

- MIT Teaching + Learning Lab on worked examples: useful for initial skill acquisition, especially for novices, and applicable even to mathematical proof when paired with self-explanation
- Retrieval Practice / Unleash Learning on interleaving: mixing closely related topics improves discrimination and long-term retention
- Agarwal and Agostinelli in *American Educator* on interleaving in math: blocked practice often looks better immediately, but interleaving wins on delayed tests because students must retrieve and choose among strategies

Adversarial check:

- Critiques of cognitive-load-theory-overreach are a fair warning here: this should not turn into passive answer study or a blanket case against inquiry. The point is guided structure early, then increasing independent proof work.
