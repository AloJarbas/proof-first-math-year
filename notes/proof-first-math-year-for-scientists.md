# A proof-first math year for scientists who want rigor without curriculum sprawl

The best first year of self-study mathematics for a scientist is not a maximal curriculum. It is a proof-first year with one active spine at a time: begin by learning how mathematical statements are written and repaired, let calculus return as the main computational thread, bring in discrete mathematics as proof reinforcement, then study linear algebra on a base that can actually sustain it.

## What matters most

### Proof-writing is the bottleneck worth attacking first

Most self-study plans fail before they reach serious mathematics because definitions, quantifiers, contradiction, and induction never become normal working tools. A proof journal solves a real problem here: it turns vague reading into finished mathematical sentences, exposes recurring errors, and makes later subjects less intimidating.

### Calculus should return early, but not as the whole story

For a scientist, calculus is too important to delay indefinitely. But if it arrives as the whole program, the year often becomes procedural again. The better role is a controlled computational spine after proof work has started to stick: enough volume to rebuild fluency, not enough to erase the proof habit.

### Resource sprawl is more dangerous than resource scarcity

The public source stack for this packet is already enough material for a year. The right operating model is simple:

- one active main text or course,
- one nearby companion,
- one weekly review ritual,
- OSSU as an audit map rather than a guilt machine.

### Linear algebra and later analysis should land on prepared ground

Linear algebra is more valuable when it becomes a study of linear maps, eigenspaces, structure, and proof, not only matrix manipulation. Analysis is the deeper maturity jump waiting on the far side of this first year. Both benefit from an earlier phase in which proof-writing is already a working habit.

## A practical year shape

### Phase 1: weeks 1-8, proof foundations

Use *Book of Proof* as the anchor. Aim for 2-4 finished proofs per week, one error log, and one timed problem block. The goal is not speed. The goal is to stop panicking when a theorem demands complete sentences.

### Phase 2: weeks 9-22, calculus as the main computational spine

Use MIT 18.01SC as the steady operational course. Keep proof work alive, but let calculus carry most of the volume. The rule is simple: reduce lecture intake before reducing problem-solving.

### Phase 3: weeks 23-34, discrete mathematics as proof reinforcement

Introduce MIT 6.042J once proof-writing and calculus rhythm are both real. Here the role of discrete mathematics is not topic collecting; it is to reinforce definitions, induction, relations, combinatorial reasoning, and clean statement handling.

### Phase 4: weeks 35-46, linear algebra

Start with MIT 18.06-style intuition and computation: systems, vector spaces, geometry, eigenideas. Then begin the shift toward a more structural view, with Axler as the natural second-pass text if throughput remains healthy.

### Phase 5: weeks 47-52, consolidation and analysis preparation

Rework weak areas, redo selected problems without notes, and write one integrative notebook entry linking the year back to physics, coding, signal processing, optics, or data analysis. The point of this block is to make analysis the honest next step rather than a symbolic aspiration.

## Weekly operating rules that matter

A workable default rhythm is six study days per week, roughly 90-120 minutes per day:

- 3 sessions for main new material,
- 2 sessions for proof or problem work,
- 1 review-and-rewrite session.

The weekly review should track only five numbers:

1. finished proofs,
2. serious problems solved,
3. definitions or theorems rewritten from memory,
4. unresolved confusion points,
5. missed or postponed study blocks.

A good first year looks slightly conservative from the outside. In practice, that is what makes the harder subjects reachable.
